Disciplinary Literacy

Understanding the specialized knowledge and abilities possessed by those who create, communicate, and use knowledge within academic disciplines

What is Disciplinary Literacy?

  • Focuses on specialized ways of reading, writing, and thinking in academic disciplines
  • Emphasizes the unique tools experts use to engage in disciplinary work
  • Goes beyond general study skills to address discipline-specific practices
  • Based on research comparing experts and novices across disciplines
  • Rooted in functional linguistics and cognitive science research

Disciplinary vs. Content Area Literacy

  • Content area literacy focuses on general study skills applicable across subjects
  • Disciplinary literacy emphasizes unique practices within each discipline
  • Content literacy = how novices approach texts
  • Disciplinary literacy = how experts create and use knowledge
  • Different approaches to vocabulary, text structure, and author awareness

Why Disciplinary Literacy Matters

  • Helps students become disciplinary insiders rather than passive consumers
  • Prepares students for college and career literacy demands
  • Addresses the unique reading challenges of different academic subjects
  • Supports the development of critical thinking within disciplines
  • Aligns with Common Core State Standards for disciplinary literacy

Key Concepts in Disciplinary Literacy

Disciplinary Literacy Definition

"An emphasis on the knowledge and abilities possessed by those who create, communicate, and use knowledge within the disciplines. The difference is that content literacy emphasizes techniques that a novice might use to make sense of a disciplinary text, whereas disciplinary literacy emphasizes the unique tools that the experts in a discipline use to engage in the work of that discipline." (Shanahan & Shanahan, 2012)

Research has revealed that disciplines differ extensively in their fundamental purposes, specialized genres, symbolic artifacts, traditions of communication, evaluation standards of quality and precision, and use of language.

Disciplinary Differences in Reading Practices

History

  • Focuses on sourcing (considering the author's perspective and bias)
  • Emphasizes contextualization (connecting texts to historical circumstances)
  • Requires corroboration (comparing across multiple sources)
  • Uses metaphorical and interpretive language

Science

  • Relies on precise technical vocabulary with Greek/Latin roots
  • Uses nominalization (turning processes into nouns like "evaporation")
  • Emphasizes classification and description of phenomena
  • Presents information through a combination of prose, equations, and graphics

Mathematics

  • Uses dense symbolic notation alongside natural language
  • Requires precision in language and logical progression
  • Emphasizes deductive reasoning and proof
  • Minimizes focus on author identity in interpretation
"The aim of disciplinary literacy is to identify all such reading- and writing-relevant distinctions among the disciplines and to find ways of teaching students to negotiate successfully these literacy aspects of the disciplines. It is an effort, ultimately, to transform students into disciplinary insiders who are able to approach literacy tasks with some sense of agency and with a set of responses and moves that are appropriate to the specialized purposes, demands, and mores of the disciplines." (Shanahan & Shanahan, 2012)
